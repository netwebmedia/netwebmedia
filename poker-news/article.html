Marius Kudzmanas Cracks Kings With 6-7 to Win Historic WSOPE Main Event

By PokerPulse Staff April 10, 2026 8 min read WSOP Europe, Prague

In one of the most dramatic finishes in World Series of Poker Europe history, Lithuanian poker player Marius Kudzmanas has become the first player from his country to win the WSOPE Main Event, taking home a staggering €2,000,000 first-place prize and the coveted gold bracelet.

The decisive hand came heads-up when Kudzmanas, holding 6-7 suited, found himself all-in against his opponent's pocket kings — a seemingly dire situation that would normally spell elimination. But the board ran out with a miraculous straight draw that completed on the river, sending the Lithuanian rail into absolute pandemonium.

The Road to the Final Table

Kudzmanas entered the final table as the short stack but played a patient, disciplined game that saw him climb steadily through the field. His aggressive three-bet strategy and well-timed bluffs kept opponents off balance throughout the final day.

"I've been working on my game for years, studying GTO theory and working with solvers every day. But in that final moment, it came down to heart. I believed in my read, I trusted my instincts, and the poker gods were on my side."
— Marius Kudzmanas, 2026 WSOPE Main Event Champion

The Main Event attracted a record-breaking field of 2,847 entries, generating a total prize pool of over €14 million. The event was held at the iconic King's Casino in Rozvadov, marking the third consecutive year WSOP Europe has called the Czech-German border venue home.

Notable Final Table Eliminations

The final table featured several notable players, including Annette Obrestad, the 2007 WSOP Europe Main Event champion, who was looking to make history with a second WSOPE title nearly two decades after her first. Obrestad's fairytale run ended in 5th place when her pocket queens ran into a set of aces.

Japanese pro Shiina Okamoto suffered the most brutal exit of the final table, running into quad sevens while holding a full house. The hand, which has already gone viral on social media, has been described as one of the worst bad beats in WSOP history.

WSOP veteran Josh Arieh, a two-time bracelet winner, saw his deep run end when his pocket aces were cracked in a massive pot, finishing in 4th place for €850,000.

Lithuania's First WSOP Gold

Kudzmanas's victory marks a historic moment for Lithuanian poker. While the Baltic nation has produced several notable tournament players, none had ever captured a WSOP bracelet prior to this event.

"This is for Lithuania," Kudzmanas said during the post-victory press conference. "I hope this inspires more players from my country to pursue their poker dreams. We have incredible talent in the Baltics, and I want to show the world what we can do."

What's Next

With his €2 million payday, Kudzmanas has vaulted into the top 50 on the All-Time Money List for European players. He told PokerPulse that he plans to use the momentum heading into the 2026 WSOP in Las Vegas this summer, where he'll be competing in multiple high-roller events and the $10,000 Main Event.

The WSOP Europe series continues with several more bracelet events through the end of the month. For complete coverage of all remaining events, follow PokerPulse's live updates on our Tournaments page.
